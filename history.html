The Chronicles of Melaka

From a humble fishing village to a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Timeline of a Kingdom

1400 – The Founding

Parameswara, a Prince from Palembang, founded Melaka after witnessing a brave mouse deer kick a hunting dog while resting under a Melaka tree. He named the city after the tree, and it soon became a strategic trading port for merchants from China, India, and the Arab world.

1511 – Portuguese Era

Alfonso de Albuquerque led the Portuguese conquest of Melaka. They built the famous fortress, A'Famosa, to consolidate their power over the spice trade route. This era introduced Catholicism and the Portuguese-Eurasian community to the region.

1641 – Dutch Rule

With the help of the Johor Sultanate, the Dutch defeated the Portuguese. Their 183-year reign left behind the iconic red Stadthuys and Christ Church buildings. Melaka became an administrative center for the Dutch East India Company (VOC).

1824 – British Influence

The Anglo-Dutch Treaty of 1824 saw Melaka ceded to the British in exchange for Bencoolen (Sumatra). Melaka became part of the Straits Settlements alongside Penang and Singapore, marking the beginning of the British colonial era.

The Golden Age of Trade

During the 15th century, Melaka was the center of the world's spice trade. It was known as the "Emporium of the East." Historical records suggest that at its peak, over 80 languages were spoken in the port of Melaka. The population was a diverse mix of Malays, Chinese, Indians, and Peranakans.

In the early 20th century, the population of Melaka was approximately 190,000. The ethnic demographic consisted of:

  • • Malays: 52% (approx. 98,800)
  • • Chinese: 38% (approx. 72,200)
  • • Indians & Others: 10% (approx. 19,000)
This diversity served as the foundation of the multicultural society we see today.

On 20 February 1956, Tunku Abdul Rahman announced the date of Malaya's independence at Padang Pahlawan in Bandar Hilir, Melaka, forever cementing the city's role in the birth of the nation.
